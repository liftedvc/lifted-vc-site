Chapter 2

What Is Authentic FOMO?

The single most important concept in this playbook. Understanding the fundamentals of Authentic FOMO is critical to your success.

Watch: The Authentic FOMO Process

Most first-time fundraisers take meetings sporadically, a few here and there across months, never hitting enough volume in one window to generate real urgency.

Authentic FOMO concentrates meetings into a short window so urgency builds naturally. Authentic is the key word: no artificial deadlines, no fake scarcity, just genuine demand any investor can verify. It describes the demand, and it describes how you show up inside it.

Bank 80–120 promised intros before anything goes out, then fire everything at once. That's what packs 30–50+ meetings into 2–3 weeks.

Authentic vs. manufactured
Hype FOMO (manufactured)
  • Artificial deadlines
  • Fake scarcity
  • Smoke and mirrors
  • Falls apart under scrutiny
Authentic FOMO
  • Genuine round traction
  • Real investor interest
  • Compressed timelines
  • Gets stronger under scrutiny

Hard Filters

Pass 1 of 2. Every investor goes through hard filters first. Fail one, they're out. No exceptions, no maybes.

Hard filters cull the list. Soft filters, which come next, tier what's left. Hard filters get you from thousands of investors down to hundreds; soft filters take you from hundreds to a focused 80–120.

An investor's decision is yes or no. Only pitch those who can say yes. Every non-disqualified investor costs hours of research, intro work, meeting prep, and follow-up. These five filters shave weeks off the raise.

The five hard filters
01
Thesis Fit 20%+ of their deals in your sector
If they don't already invest in your space, they'd have to learn an entire market to back you. Small funds: 3+ deals in your space.
02
Stage Fit 15%+ of their deals at your stage
Stage-mismatched meetings happen for the investor's benchmarking. They take the meeting to learn, not to write a check.
03
Geography 5%+ of deals in your market
Early-stage VCs prefer portfolio companies in their time zone, on their LPs' regulatory map, reachable in person.
04
Portfolio Conflicts No direct competitors
Adjacent is fine; direct is not. Same customer, same problem, same business model. VCs almost never bet against their portfolio.
05
Investment Velocity 1+ deal in the last 6 months
Quiet funds are often out of capital. They'll take meetings to look active and never write a check. 12+ months of silence is a hard fail.

Soft Filters

Pass 2 of 2. Soft filters don't eliminate investors, they decide where you spend effort. Your working list of 80–120 is Tier 1 and Tier 2 only.

Tier 1 investors get most of your effort. Tier 2 get some. Tier 3 is backstop only and doesn't count toward your target.

Tier 1 · passes all
70%
High-conviction. Strong intro, scheduled early.
Tier 2 · fails one
25%
Likely follow-ons; a lead can persuade them.
Tier 3 · fails 2+
5%
Backstop only. Pitch if a connector volunteers.
Aim for ~70% of your working list to be Tier 1. Tier 3 may be the largest group on your spreadsheet, but they get about 5% of your effort.
The five soft filters
$
Check Size 1–2% of fund size per check
A $50M fund usually writes ~$500K checks. Use as a signal, not a rule. Compelling deals still break patterns.
V
Valuation Range yellow flag
If recent deals are priced at 2× your expected valuation, deprioritize. Don't remove.
M
Target Markets yellow flag
B2B vs B2C, enterprise vs SMB. A portfolio all over the map is a yellow flag. You want a clear pattern that includes you.
T
Traction Bar yellow flag
If comparable deals they backed required 10× your current traction, they may be waiting for more.
L
Lead Appetite critical for leads
Need a lead? Target investors who've led 10%+ of their deals (20% is strong). At least 30% of your Tier 1 should be lead candidates. Angels and family offices are almost always follow-on.

Chapter 6

Why Warm Intros Win

A warm intro is more than an open door. Done well, it carries you from the first meeting all the way through due diligence.

Watch: Why warm intros win

Getting a meeting is not the goal. Getting a check is. A warm intro is what keeps you moving toward one: when a founder the investor trusts vouches for you, that vouch works as credibility, social proof, and the first data point the investor uses to evaluate you. Cold outreach starts each of those from scratch.

The math makes the case
Cold outreach to an investor
~1–3% reply rate
Cold is any approach without a trusted connector: cold email, website contact forms, the fund’s general inbox, LinkedIn or Twitter DMs to a partner. You’re competing on luck in an inbox full of hundreds of other strangers. Zero social proof at the first touchpoint: the intro is the first data point, and you just delivered a blank one.
Warm intro through a real connector
~30–40% reply rate
Roughly 10× better, and the gap compounds at every funnel stage. The connector is staking their own reputation on you, which is a form of due diligence. Knowing how to get to someone is the same skill you’ll use to close customers, hire operators, and build a company.
The path is the first data point

Investors pattern-match constantly. Before they read your deck, they read how you arrived. A portfolio founder they trust? They lean in. An operator they respect? Same. A cold email? They read it as spray-and-pray, as a founder who doesn’t understand how venture works, or as lazy. In most cases, it ends up in a general inbox monitored by a junior VC who is just trying to get to inbox zero as quickly as possible.

Two narrow exceptions. (1) Cold outreach to founders (not investors) has a decent conversion rate, as high as 5–20% through LinkedIn DMs, when you’re working to earn a connector and warm intro. (2) Funds or programs that require an application form (e.g. accelerators, application-based funds). Outside those two, everything else is wasted cycles.
Chapter 6

What a Connector Is

A connector is someone with genuine, warm relationships to partners at your target funds. Not a LinkedIn connection, not someone who "knows of" the firm. The partner would recognize and respond to them.

Watch: What a connector is
Three tiers of connector · Tier B is your primary pipeline
Tier A · most powerful
Existing investors
People already on your cap table, actively advocating for you. They know how to intro and they want you to raise. No workflow needed — go direct and ask.
Tier B · primary pipeline
Portfolio founders of the fund
Founders the fund has backed. Ideally, they have just raised additional capital or had a positive exit. This will likely be the primary source of your introductions.
Tier C · avoid
Service providers & casual contacts
Lawyers, accountants, service providers, and advisors who haven’t invested. Skip them unless the relationship is exceptional. Investors know they are being compensated.
A single well-connected individual who can intro you to 5+ investors can help accelerate your warm introduction mining. When you find one, treat them like gold. But also make sure to vet their relationship with the investors, as high volume can sometimes mean a shallow connection.
If your connector has no real pull with the partner (no track record, no recent conversation, no specific relationship), the intro is low leverage because the connector is effectively coming in cold themselves.

Chapter 6 · Map Your Connectors

Who to Target at the Fund

There is really only one target: Partners. 99% of the time, only partners write checks. Everyone else at the fund routes, screens, or assists.

Watch: Who to target at the fund
Two categories · one worth pursuing
Target
Partners (GP)
Partners are the ones who write checks and carry the most weight when championing deals internally. They are the ones you need to sign off on the deal. Your connector’s credibility needs to be meaningful to the partner themself, and it won’t if your connector doesn’t know them.
Deflect
Junior VC (Associate, Analyst, Principal, VP)
They source, screen, and filter. Some can champion internally, but none can write a check or carry a deal through the partnership meeting alone. An intro to a junior starts you on the filter side of the funnel rather than the decision side. Redirect.
What to say when you’re offered a junior intro
“Thanks so much. Out of respect for everyone’s time, I try to get introduced directly to the partner who covers [sector/stage] at [Firm]. Is there a specific partner you’d recommend, or someone who could make that intro?”

The move: acknowledge warmly so you don’t burn the connector, name the reason (respecting everyone’s time), ask for the partner specifically, and put the ball back on the connector to facilitate.

Narrow exception: if fund fit is genuinely unclear, a junior meeting works as qualification plus pitch practice. Take it, pursue a parallel partner intro, and downgrade the fund in your list if the only entry remains through the junior.
When a partner has already taken the meeting and hands you to an associate for follow-up diligence, that’s standard process, not a filter. The partner is still your champion; the associate is doing the workflow. Different from a cold junior entry.

From Chapter 5

Target
80–100 at 2B or better

Exception
Already have a lead?

What counts
1A, 1B, 2A, 2B

Example: what hitting the bar looks like

You have 100 investors on your list with warm introductions mapped. From Chapter 5, 70% are Tier 1 and 25% are Tier 2. That gives you 70 pairings at 1A or 1B and 25 at 2A or 2B, for 95 at 2B or better. That’s the quality bar your list needs to hit.

If the list feels unsure, it’s underbaked

Cold outreach is the wrong answer when the list doesn’t hit the bar. Spending two extra weeks earning high-quality warm intros almost always saves months of a slow, stalled raise later.

If the list feels unsure, it’s underbaked. Keep building connector coverage until the bar feels comfortable, not aspirational.

The Double Opt-In System

The four-step mechanism that underlies every warm intro. It protects the connector, respects the investor, and lets you control timing.

Watch: The double opt-in system
The four steps
Step 01
Get the commitment
Connector verbally agrees to forward your blurb to a specific named partner.
Step 02
Connector forwards
They send your blurb with personal context. This is where credibility transfers.
Step 03
Investor opts in
Investor confirms they're open. Only then does the formal intro happen.
Step 04
Schedule on your terms
You pick when, based on round sequencing (leads first, follow-ons later).

The system only works if you have a sharp blurb. Chapter 3 covers writing one. Draft it before you start making connector calls. And because the investor doesn't get the intro until you tell the connector to forward, you control timing. That's the lever.

Double opt-in is your timing lever for the whole raise. Connectors confirm the intro and wait for your green light, so nothing forwards until you say so. The round stays dark while you stage 80–120 promises. Then you release every connector over the course of a few days: intros land in parallel, meetings compress into 2–3 weeks, and Authentic FOMO emerges because the activity is real.

Chapter 7 · Activate Your Intros

Connector Calls

Most of your connectors are Tier B portfolio founders. Every call has one specific outcome: a commitment to forward your blurb to a named partner.

Watch: Connector calls
Booking the call · three principles
01 · Intro first
Get a warm intro to the founder
Intros earn credibility. Cold-emailing portfolio founders works less than half as well as a referral from a mutual connection.
02 · Send materials
Share your deck before the call
Don't make them learn about you on the call itself. Deck or one-pager ahead of time so they show up with context.
03 · Frame softly
Ask for their perspective, not an intro
"I'd love your perspective on our approach" is easier to say yes to than "can you intro me to your investors?"
Running the call
Do
  • Name the fund and the partner. Specificity separates a real ask from a vague one.
  • Give a concise, confident picture. Arm them so the intro email writes itself.
  • Make the de-escalated ask. "Will you forward an email to the investor to see if they’re interested in a meeting?" Easier to say yes to than "will you make the intro?"
  • Confirm they know the investor well. A weak intro can hurt more than no intro.
  • Bring value. A useful connection, a sharp insight, honest product feedback.
Don't
  • Pitch extensively. You're asking, not pitching. Keep the company overview tight.
  • Ask for the intro before the blurb forward. Blurb commitment first, then build.
  • Ask "who do you know?" Always name the investor. Open-ended questions get useless answers.

What "promised" actually means

Vague enthusiasm isn't a promise. "I'll see what I can do" or "Send me your blurb and I'll forward to some investors" with no named investor means no real commitment.

A promise is explicit: agreement to forward your blurb to a specific named partner, connector understands their role, you've confirmed they know the investor well, logged in CRM with name + investor + date. If you leave a call unsure, you don't have one. Log as hedged, send one follow-up, and move on.

End every call with one question: "Who else do you know, investors or founders who've raised from funds in our space?" One conversation can multiply your network by 10 to 15x. The founders who do this consistently end up with twice as many promised intros as the founders who don't.

A one-minute story that makes the problem you solve and how you solve it impossible to miss. Every material you ship after is a translation of it, so get it ruthlessly clear here.

The three pillars · what a great one-minute pitch lands
The problem is real
The investor needs to understand this before all else. Use human evidence, real stories, and quantified pain to show the problem happening in the real world, today.

One-Pager

One-pagers are an efficient way to communicate the details of your company. A lot of investors actually prefer them to decks, since the full picture lands in 60 seconds rather than 20 slides.

Research from NFX Capital shows one-pagers get 2.5× higher response rates than full decks for cold outreach. A document that takes 60 seconds to read gets read. A 20-slide deck gets skipped.

The 7 Sections: Highlight your strongest assets.
01
Company Headline
One sentence: what you do, who you serve, and the core benefit. Investors decide in 5 seconds whether to keep reading. Follow with a traction hook (“$100K MRR, 30% MoM”) or a team hook (“Ex-Google engineer + 15-year industry operator”).
02
Problem
2–3 sentences. Quantify the pain. Dollars lost, failure rates, people affected. Small problems stay small. Make it obvious why this needs to exist.
03
Solution
2–3 sentences directly addressing the problem. Include your unique insight: why previous approaches fell short and why yours works. Use customer validation language where you have it.
04
Traction
Lead with your most impressive metric in large, bold text: revenue, user count, or growth rate. Show monthly progression. Include retention if strong. No traction yet? Highlight founder-market fit and domain expertise.
05
Market
TAM with transparent math (“10M restaurants × $1,200/year = $12B market”). Follow with your entry wedge: where you start, how you expand. This signals go-to-market sophistication.
06
Team
2–3 sentences on relevant experience and past wins. Complementary skills and prior collaboration matter. For early-stage companies, team is often what investors key in on first.
07
The Ask
Be specific: amount, instrument (SAFE, priced round), post-money valuation, and how much is already committed. Specificity signals process. Follow with a brief use-of-funds breakdown tied to milestones. This shows you’re raising against a plan.
Exit scenarios: signals your focus is already on the exit rather than the build. Round naming (“Pre-seed”, “Series A”): state the dollar amount and terms instead. Detailed financials: key metrics belong here, full models live in the data room. Anything beyond the hook: if it earns the meeting, it belongs. Everything else stays in the deck or the data room for later.
Tiny font: if you’re below 10pt, you’re cramming. White space earns its place. Generic language: specific beats vague every time. “$12B market growing 15% annually” proves the market. “Large market opportunity” proves nothing. Wrong ordering: lead with strength. Burying your best asset under sections you felt were expected is a common and costly mistake. Missing the ask: a specific ask signals you’re running a real process. Why Practice Is Non-Negotiable ▼

First meetings are typically 30 minutes: ~5 min intros, optional 5-min quick pitch, ~20 min Q&A. The deck sets the content trajectory. Good founders drive the meeting on their terms.

How to Practice
How Much Practice You Actually Need ▼

Your pitch doesn't need to be perfect before you start. It needs to be good enough that you're comfortable. So your energy goes into the conversation, not the content. The problem is what every pitch is built around. The investor needs to internalize it before the rest of the pitch can land.

Watch: The Problem
Four elements every great problem statement shares

Experienced fundraisers spend a disproportionate amount of time working on how they pitch the problem. It leads off in most collateral, and often the first subject of a meeting. The instinct is to rush to the solution because that’s the exciting part, but it’s the problem that makes your solution interesting. The best problem statements share the same four elements. Build all four into your pitch and the investor sees the problem through your eyes.

Element 01
A specific scene
Anchor in a specific subject. A person, team, department, or company in a clear context. Specific scenes tend to land. Investors connect to something they can picture, then extrapolate to the market on their own. The strongest framings draw a clear line: inside the system something works, outside the system it breaks.
Element 02
The customer’s POV
Tell the problem the way the customer lives it. Use their language, their texture, their experience. Even without a direct quote, staying in their frame helps investors see the problem more clearly. Also, if you have them, verbatim quotes help take it even deeper.
Element 03
The cost and scope
Show both the per-customer cost and how widespread the problem is. Hours burned, dollars lost, deals missed, customers churned. The number of subjects feeling the same pain, and what they collectively spend dealing with it. Also, establishing the scope here helps clarify your market size later in the pitch.
Element 04
The band-aid solutions
Walk through what customers do today to manage the problem. Hacky spreadsheets, dedicated headcount, third-party tools that almost work, manual workarounds that break at scale. The silliness of those band-aids does double duty. It proves how badly customers want a fix, and sets up the wedge for your solution.

Five principles for sizing your market
Principle 01
Skip TAM, SAM, SOM
The acronyms encourage top-down thinking. The market-share assumption at the end tends to be an unsubstantiated afterthought, which leaves you with a number that doesn’t mean much to investors.
Principle 02
Build it bottom up
Multiply the number of customers by the average revenue per customer per year. For transactional businesses, use transaction volume times price. The assumptions get substantiated by the rest of your pitch: customer definition, revenue model, GTM. Bottom-up is the version investors can pressure-test.
Principle 03
Show 5+ years of future revenue
Data from a Series A VC suggests that future revenue matters more than current size, even at seed. A bottom-up build of revenue five-plus years out tends to land harder than a hypothetical percentage share of TAM or SAM.
Principle 04
Be realistic about market share
Pitch decks often claim a 10% market share. That assumption tends to be anchored in the consumer FAANG world, where dominant platforms exist. Most categories are more fragmented or under-penetrated, so pick a number you can justify with bottom-up math.
Principle 05
Clarity over the number
Clarity of thought and enduring ambition matter more than the headline market-size figure itself. The goal of the section is to show you understand the market deeply, and you’re targeting a big opportunity inside it. The number is downstream of that.

Your Traction

Investors anchor on traction more than almost anything else in your pitch. Which isn’t surprising, because traction is an objective proof point. The strongest move is to lead with clarity here. The accurate number with the correct label resonates more with investors.

Watch: Your Traction
Lead with clarity

The instinct for founders is to make traction sound as strong as possible, and do things like lump pilots, LOIs, and run rate into a single “ARR” number, or stretch definitions to make the headline pop. But because this is a proof point for investors, the strongest move is to lead with clarity.

A founder who claims “$600K ARR” in a first meeting after annualizing one strong $50K month loses credibility for the rest of the deal. Be accurate with whatever revenue you’re claiming. Saying “$50K in revenue last month” from the start keeps the numbers real and the deal moving.

Use the right label for the right number

The strongest evidence is real paid revenue. Whether that’s MRR for a SaaS company or run rate for a transactional one is determined by your business model. Bookings, GMV, and LOIs follow, ranked from strongest to weakest.

01
Actual paid revenue
Real dollars invoiced and paid. The strongest evidence regardless of business model. Pick the label that fits how your revenue works.
SaaS or subscription · MRR / ARR. Revenue invoiced and paid on a contract that renews monthly or yearly. Phrase it as: “$50K MRR” or “$600K ARR.”
Transactional · Run rate. Actual sales annualized. If monthly sales are running around $50K, your annual run rate is $600K. Phrase it as: “$50K per month in transactional revenue” or “$600K annual run rate.” ARR is reserved for revenue that actually recurs by contract.
02
Bookings / cARR
A signed contract specifying $X per year or per month. Stronger than an LOI. Pilots, opt-out clauses, and “starts billing after deployment” all reduce its strength. Phrase it as: “$100K in cARR, mostly pilots that start billing once we’re fully deployed.”
03
GMV / GSR
For marketplaces and retailers, this is the top-line transaction volume flowing through your platform. Your revenue is GMV multiplied by your take rate. Phrase it as: “$100K GMV this month at a 20% take rate, so $20K in marketplace revenue.”
04
Non-binding LOIs
A signed letter saying a customer would pay $X if your product gets built and works. An indication of intent, with no payment attached. Phrase it as: “$100K in non-binding LOIs from customers X, Y, and Z.”

The Competition

How a founder talks about competition is one of the fastest signals investors get about market awareness, intellectual honesty, and strategic clarity. The way you map the field, position against incumbents, and explain why you win tells investors how you think.

Watch: The Competition
Sources
Best practices on this page draw from Y Combinator (Kevin Hale, Michael Seibel), First Round Capital (Josh Kopelman), Sequoia Capital, Andreessen Horowitz, and Hustle Fund. The moat taxonomy and the data-network-effects skepticism trace to Andreessen Horowitz’s writing on defensibility. The speed-of-execution framing for pre-seed companies comes from Hustle Fund. The single test

Can the investor explain how you win, in their own words? Everything below is in service of that one question.

Five moves to build your competitive narrative

Each move adds a layer to the case. Build all five and the investor leaves the meeting able to map the field and trace your edge to a specific reason. Use the arrows to step through.

01
Map the field, including the status quo

The single biggest unforced error is claiming you have no competition. Map direct competitors (same problem, same audience, similar approach) and indirect ones (same pain through a different mechanism, plus the status quo). For most pre-seed companies, the largest competitor is “people doing nothing,” “a spreadsheet,” or “an internal tool somebody built five years ago.” Naming the status quo often does more work than any logo grid.

02
Lead with a durable differentiator, not a feature

Features investors see as easy to replicate signal the company hasn’t found its real edge yet. Durable differentiators sit deeper: technology depth, distribution advantage, positioning, brand, go-to-market mechanics, team composition, regulatory access, or a unique insight about the customer. Features can support these; the sustainable advantage runs deeper than any single feature.

03
Tie differentiation to your unique insight

The most credible competitive narratives turn the unique insight into the reason your differentiation exists. The story sounds like: “Here’s what incumbents are doing. Here’s what we believe they got wrong, based on these specific customer signals. That belief led us to build differently in this specific way, which is why we win in this segment first.” Competition becomes the proof point for the insight.

04
Make the defensibility case explicit

Investors know your moat is thin today. Their question is whether you have a credible path to defensibility as you scale. Pick one or two of the four classic mechanisms (economies of scale, network effects, switching costs, counter-positioning) that genuinely apply, and walk the investor through how each strengthens with scale. Be precise about network effects especially. Much of what founders call data network effects is closer to data scale effects, where the marginal value of each new data point falls as the cost of acquiring unique data rises. Claim only the defensibility you can prove, and spell out the mechanics: walk through the feedback loop, name the participant type, name the specific value each new participant adds. Vague “our moat is execution” claims land flat.

05
Anticipate the standard threats

Two competitive threats come up almost every time: “What if a well-funded competitor copies your feature tomorrow?” and “What stops a big incumbent from waking up?” Have specific answers ready, and pre-empt the well-funded copycat question yourself when you can; raising it first turns a doubt into evidence of self-awareness. The strongest answers separate the feature from the underlying advantage, or name structural reasons (failed incumbent attempts, internal incentives, channel conflict) the threat usually doesn’t materialize. Keep a sharp 15-second version of the answer ready for warm intros and offline conversations, where an honest, specific reply travels furthest.

A valid contrarian view

Some pre-seed investors push back on the standard competitive playbook and argue you should make execution speed and learning velocity the centerpiece of the narrative, with everything else playing a supporting role. The case for it: at pre-seed, speed compounds faster than any other advantage you can credibly claim, and founders who lead with it tend to outrun a field that put defensibility first.

The Ask

The ask is the moment a fundraising conversation turns from storytelling into a transaction. Founders who skip it leave deals on the table every meeting.

Sources
Best practices on this page draw from Y Combinator (Michael Seibel, Kevin Hale, Aaron Harris), First Round Capital, Sequoia Capital, Andreessen Horowitz (Scott Kupor), and Hustle Fund. The “actually ask” framing and the milestone-over-hires guidance trace to Seibel’s “How to Pitch Your Company.” The valuation, tweener, and run-a-process-that-creates-competition framings come from Scott Kupor at Andreessen Horowitz. The single test

Did you actually ask, name the amount, tie it to a milestone, and request a decision? Everything below is in service of that one question.

Six moves to build your ask

Each move adds a layer. Build all six and a specific ask reads as someone who has been thoughtful and capital efficient.

Move 01
Actually ask
Most founders dance around the ask, hoping the investor will offer unprompted. They won’t. The closing question must be direct, specific, and force a yes-or-no answer. “We’re raising $2M on a SAFE at $15M post. $1.4M committed. Closing in three weeks. Are you in?”
Move 02
Tie the ask to milestones, not time or hires
Investors fund the next valuation inflection, not runway or hiring plans. “$1.5M to go from $30K MRR to $150K MRR over 18 months” beats both “$1.5M for 18 months of runway” and “$1.5M to hire three engineers and two AEs.” Hiring is a means to an end. Lead with the outcome.
Move 03
Size the round for the next valuation inflection
The dollar amount should map to a believable plan that delivers a credible next-round story. The standard runway target holds: 18 to 24 months. Raising takes about six months; hitting milestones that make the next round fundable takes 12 to 18 of execution. Less than 18 puts the company back in market before there are meaningful proof points.
Move 04
Show use of funds simply
A one-pager with three columns: percentage allocation, what that bucket buys, the milestone it unlocks. “60% engineering and product to ship V2 and unlock enterprise pipeline. 25% go-to-market to reach $1M ARR. 15% reserve.” Investors want to see the dollars are tied to outcomes, not a line-by-line budget.
Move 05
Don’t optimize for the highest valuation
The highest headline number isn’t the best outcome. A valuation set too high creates pressure to grow into it within the runway you have, and missing that bar leads to flat or down rounds. Lead with the amount and the milestones. Treat valuation as a downstream negotiation, not an opening anchor.
Move 06
Run a process that creates competition
The ask lands harder when investors know other investors are interested. Batch first meetings into a tight two-to-three week window. Be transparent about timing. Share progress signals as they happen. State the facts of your process without hyperbole.

Intro email. The ask appears plainly in the email, not buried in an attachment. “We’re raising $2M on a SAFE at $15M post. $1.2M committed. Closing in four weeks. Would you like to take a look?”

First call. End every first call with a direct question. “Based on what you’ve seen, what would need to be true for you to lead this round?” Force a real answer. Vague signals are noes.

Partner meeting. The ask should be tight and the use of funds defensible. Walk through the milestone math, the runway logic, and the next-round thesis without notes.

Follow-up. Anything but a check is a no. Drive toward a decision rather than restating the pitch. “We’re tracking to close on Friday and would love to know whether to count you in.”

Investor update. Updates after the round closes are how founders set up the next ask. Each update reinforces the milestone story that justified the last round and the trajectory that will justify the next one.

Offline conversations and warm intros. A 15-second version of the ask in your back pocket at all times. “We’re raising a $2M seed to reach $1M ARR. Already at $30K MRR with strong enterprise pipeline. Closing in March.”

Chapter 8

Pitching Best Practices

The craft of a first meeting. Knowing the three acts, when to lead, and how to run the parts that are yours changes the odds of every pitch you give.

Watch: Anatomy of a first meeting

Almost every first meeting follows the same shape. The investor opens with their fund pitch, you take the floor and run the next 15 to 20 minutes, and the final chunk is Q&A. Knowing where you are in the arc and what each act needs from you is the difference between a meeting you run and a meeting that runs over you.

The three acts · 30 minutes, predictable shape
Act 1 · ~5 min
Their turn
The investor pitches the fund and the thesis. Don’t try to shortcut it. Listen actively, note one specific thing they said you’ll reference later.
Act 2 · ~15 to 20 min
Your turn
The power-dynamic handoff. Acknowledge their thesis in one sentence, then bridge to the problem and run your pitch on your terms.
Act 3 · ~5 to 10 min
Q&A and close
The meeting you came for. Answer conclusion-first, keep it specific, and leave them with a concrete next step and timeline.
The handoff · the most important transition in the meeting

The moment you take the floor sets the tone for the next 20 minutes. Skip the investor’s intro and you signal you only care about your pitch. Acknowledge their thesis in one sentence and you earn credibility to run the rest. Something like: “Love the thesis on operational AI for mid-market. That’s exactly what we’re tackling. Here’s what’s happening on the ground.” Then go.

Chapter 8 · Pitching Best Practices

Your Energy Sets the Room’s Energy

Investors pattern-match on energy before they pattern-match on anything else. Over-prepared reads as artifice. Show up rested, present, and believing.

Watch: Your energy sets the room
Vibe check over script · how over-preparing kills you

Founders who rehearse the deck obsessively start sounding like a TED talk. The investor feels the artifice and checks out. The goal is to know the fundamentals, not the words. When you stop thinking about the sentence you’re delivering, your conviction about the business starts to show through instead.

The best founders sound like they’re having a conversation, not delivering prepared remarks. If you show up exhausted or scripted, the investor will feel it. If you show up with authentic energy and belief, they tend to follow.

Survival brain · getting present before the meeting

Urgency and stress responses block real investor conversations. Getting out of survival brain before a meeting matters more than one more deck rehearsal. When you’re fighting for your life in the meeting, the investor reads it as low conviction. When you’re present and grounded, your story lands.

The founder is most of what’s being evaluated
At pre-seed and seed, the founder is most of what an investor is evaluating. Your judgment, your demeanor, your energy, and the way you build relationships all sit alongside the deck and the traction numbers. Every interaction tends to land as a data point. The most reliable thing you can do is keep showing up as yourself, because polish is something the materials and the practice can carry for you.
Stand, breathe, drop your shoulders. Name what you want the investor to feel by the end of the meeting, not what you want to tell them. Then remember one customer who told you this mattered. Walk in believing your own story rather than defending it.

Lead With the Problem

Make the investor fall in love with the problem before you touch the solution. Slow the tempo, use specific people and real stories, and hold longer than feels comfortable.

The problem is the anchor

If the investor doesn’t buy the problem, the solution becomes noise and the business model becomes irrelevant. This is why the best pitches spend a disproportionate amount of time on the problem, sometimes three to five slides in a deck, always the first full thought of the meeting.

Too many founders rush to the solution because they’re excited about what they’re building. The opposite of what lands. Invest the time to make the investor see through your eyes why this problem drives its victims nuts.

Specific beats abstract · every time
Human evidence
A real person in a real moment feeling the pain. Not “customers struggle with X.” A named operator at a specific time of day doing something that breaks.
Quantified suffering
Dollars lost, hours burned, outcomes missed. A number the investor can put in the model for themselves.
Quoted customers
War-story language from real conversations, not sanitized. “Here’s what she said verbatim when we asked.”
When you feel the urge to speed past the problem because you’re excited to explain the solution, slow down instead. The investor needs to feel the pain before the solution has somewhere to land. Uncomfortable pauses in the problem section work in your favor.
Chapter 8 · Pitching Best Practices

Pyramid Answers and the Five Messages

Answer conclusion-first. Build a five-word message map. Anchor every Q&A answer back to it.

Watch: Pyramid answers and the five messages
Pyramid answers · conclusion first, detail after

Investors make split-second decisions about whether to keep listening. If they don’t hear the answer in the first five seconds, they’re already thinking about their next meeting. Every Q&A answer leads with the conclusion, then layers in the supporting detail only if asked.

Question: “How big is your market?”

Pyramid answer: “$200B annually. Mid-market banks spending on SOC audits alone. Happy to walk you through the build if useful.”

The anti-pattern: five minutes of methodology, a dozen caveats, and finally the number buried in the last sentence. The investor has already moved on.

Question: “What’s your revenue?”

Pyramid answer: “$50K MRR growing 15% month over month, with another $100K in cARR from pilots that start billing this quarter.”

The anti-pattern: a single inflated number that mixes LOIs, pilots, and recurring revenue under one label. Investors pattern-match on the precision of the answer, and the credibility hit lasts the rest of the meeting. Use the right label for each layer (see Materials › Best Practices › Be precise about your traction).

The five-message framework · a message map you can anchor every answer to

Identify the five key messages an investor needs to leave the meeting with. Each one stripped down to a single word or number. Then every Q&A answer anchors back to one of the five. Example:

Message 01
TAM · $200B
Message 02
Customer · Mid-market banks
Message 03
Problem · SOC audits take 40 hours
Message 04
Wedge · OCC rule, Q3 deadline
Message 05
Traction · 3 design partners live

If your team can’t say the five messages out loud from memory, you don’t know them well enough yet. Run the exercise before every pitch cycle.

Plain English, every word. Strip marketing speak, AI phrasing, and TED-talk cadence. Present tense only (“we are doing X,” not “we will”). Precision over range (“$2.5M,” not “$2 to 3M”). Know your numbers cold. One number reads as thought. A range reads as uncertainty.

Reading the Room

Different investor phrases mean different things. Decoding what you hear tells you whether to push, hold, or move on.

Watch: Reading the room
Four types of rejection · each calls for a different move
Specific rejection
A real thesis gap, actionable
“Sales cycle feels long.” “Team capacity on eng looks thin.” Honest, specific feedback tells you exactly what to address. Response: fix the gap, re-engage with new data.
Vague rejection
Capital or logistics, not your deal
“Recent developments in our pipeline.” “Timing isn’t right.” The investor is constrained or distracted. Response: stay on their radar, don’t push. They may re-engage later.
Ghosting
Process failed on their side
No response, no follow-up. The deal fell through their filter or bounced to the wrong partner. Response: light follow-up, consider a warm re-intro from a mutual contact.
Re-engagement ask
FOMO trigger, not real feedback
“Prove traction first.” “Come back when you have a lead.” They’re interested but want someone else to move first. Response: build momentum elsewhere and let them feel it.
Decode the common phrases

“Come back when you have a lead” = No, not maybe. They’re not impressed enough to move. Treat it as a no-now and move on.

“We’re interested, just waiting for someone else to go first” = Low conviction. If they truly believed, they’d tell you what they need to see next and when.

“Prove traction first” = They want FOMO, not proof. Build it elsewhere.

“Here’s what we need to see. Let’s check in six weeks.” = Real interest. Specific, time-bound, named. Keep them warm.

Three things to leave them with
01

Not “let’s chat soon.” Name the artifact you’ll send, the follow-up conversation you want, and who you’re asking to bring into the next one.

Specific follow-up questions about diligence. Named references to other partners they want you to meet. Specific dollar amounts mentioned. Offers to introduce you to someone. Asks about your round timeline.

Cool signals

“We’ll be in touch.” “Interesting but not quite a fit right now.” “Come back once you have a lead.” Generic questions about the market instead of specific ones about your company. No reference to timing or process.

Key Terms to Understand Before a Lead Comes In ▼

This isn't about negotiating tactics yet. That's Chapter 10. This is vocabulary. Know it cold before you need it.

1
Valuation & Dilution
Pre-money valuation sets the price; dilution is how much ownership you give up. It compounds across future rounds. Model it out before you agree to anything.
2
Pro Rata Rights
Investor's right to participate in future rounds to maintain their ownership %. Standard at seed. Understand what you're granting and to whom.
3
Board Composition
Who gets a board seat. At early stages, fight to maintain founder control. Every seat you give away is leverage you don't get back.
4
Protective Provisions
Investor veto rights on specific decisions: fundraises, acquisitions, major spending. Standard, but worth understanding exactly what you're agreeing to.
5
Option Pool
Equity set aside for future employee grants. Often created at raise time, diluting founders before the investor comes in. Negotiate the size carefully.
6
Liquidation Preference
Payout order if the company is sold. 1x non-participating is standard and founder-friendly. Anything more aggressive (2x, participating preferred) warrants scrutiny.

Close the Round

Secure the lead, fill the rest, negotiate with confidence, and get the wire.

Lead First, Then Fill ▼

You cannot fill a round without a lead. The lead sets the terms, leads diligence, and signals credibility to every follow-on investor. Getting to the first yes is the hardest part.

Once you have a lead, the dynamic of every follow-on conversation changes completely. Your Tier 1 list should have been built around lead investors first. Follow-on investors can be engaged earlier. They often want in before the lead is locked so they're not crowded out later.

Filling the Rest of the Round ▼

With a signed term sheet, you're managing allocation. Not selling the deal. The dynamics shift entirely.

  • Re-engage Tier 2 and Tier 3 pipeline. Investors who passed or went quiet often reverse on a credible term sheet
  • Activate angels and small funds. They move faster and are less sensitive to valuation
  • Set a clear allocation framework. Know how much room you have and who gets priority
  • Create urgency that's real "We're closing the round in X weeks" is more effective when it's true
Negotiation Principles ▼
1
Valuation is only one term
Pro rata rights, board composition, information rights, and protective provisions all matter. A high valuation with bad terms can hurt you more than a lower one with clean terms.
2
Leverage = competition
This is why you aimed for multiple term sheets in Chapter 9. Without a competing offer, you're negotiating from zero leverage.
3
Know your BATNA
What's your best alternative if this deal falls through? Knowing that clearly makes you a better negotiator. Ambiguity breeds concessions.
4
A solid deal now beats a perfect deal later
Optimize within realistic bounds. Chasing the ideal that may not materialize costs time and credibility.
5
Get a lawyer. Always.
Every term sheet. Founders who skip this step almost always regret it. Terms are legally binding and compounding.

When the Round Doesn't Come Together ▼

Not every raise closes the way it was planned. The honest version of this chapter includes what to do when the process stalls.

  • Seed extensions are sometimes the right move. If you're close to Series A milestones but not quite there, a bridge or seed extension keeps the company alive. The CEO's primary job is to keep the company alive. Do what the situation requires.
  • Venture debt is an option with conditions. Requires proven unit economics, an established revenue model, and ARR minimums from debt partners. Not available to everyone, but worth evaluating.

Sales & Growth Glossary

For pre-seed, seed, and Series A founders. Skim the categories or search the page with Cmd+F.

Revenue & Recurring Metrics
ARR · Annual Recurring Revenue

The yearly run-rate of predictable subscription revenue. Multiply MRR by 12. Investors anchor on this for SaaS valuation, often paying multiples on it directly.

MRR · Monthly Recurring Revenue

Predictable subscription revenue normalized to a one-month period. The heartbeat metric for SaaS. Track new MRR, expansion MRR, churned MRR, and contraction MRR separately.

TCV · Total Contract Value

The full dollar value of a contract over its entire term, including one-time fees plus all recurring revenue. Useful for measuring deal size, less useful for valuation.

ACV · Annual Contract Value

The annualized portion of contract revenue. On a three-year, $300k contract, ACV is $100k. The standard input for SaaS sales productivity math.

ARPU · Average Revenue Per User

Total revenue divided by total users in a period. The default for consumer products and freemium PLG businesses.

ARPA · Average Revenue Per Account

Total revenue divided by total customer accounts. The right denominator for B2B businesses where one account contains many seats.

GMV · Gross Merchandise Value

Total dollar value of goods or services sold through a marketplace before fees and refunds. Investors look at GMV plus take rate to back into actual revenue.

GSR · Gross Sales Revenue

Total revenue booked before discounts, returns, allowances, or refunds are applied. The top of the revenue waterfall.

NRR · Net Revenue Retention

Revenue retained from existing customers, with expansion added back. Above 100% means the existing book grows on its own. Best-in-class SaaS sits at 120%+.

GRR · Gross Revenue Retention

Revenue retained from existing customers, measured before any expansion is added back. Caps at 100% and reveals raw churn pressure on the book.

Unit Economics
LTV · Lifetime Value (also CLV)

The total gross profit a customer generates over the full relationship. For SaaS, a common shortcut is ARPA times gross margin divided by churn rate.

CAC · Customer Acquisition Cost

Total sales and marketing spend in a period divided by new customers acquired in that period. Include fully-loaded headcount costs to avoid flattering yourself.

LTV:CAC · Lifetime Value to Acquisition Cost Ratio

The classic unit economics ratio. 3:1 is the rule of thumb for healthy SaaS. Below that signals an efficiency problem; well above can signal you should spend more.

CAC Payback · CAC Payback Period

Months required for the gross profit from a new customer to repay the CAC. Sub-12 months reads as efficient at seed and Series A; sub-18 is acceptable for enterprise.

COGS · Cost of Goods Sold

Direct costs of delivering the product. For SaaS, primarily hosting, third-party services, payment processing, and customer support. Drives gross margin.

Gross Margin

Revenue minus COGS, expressed as a percentage of revenue. SaaS investors expect 70%+ at scale. Below 60% raises questions about whether the business is software at all.

Market & Funnel
ICP · Ideal Customer Profile

The specific type of customer who gets the most value from the product, closes fastest, retains longest, and refers others. Sharper ICP, faster sales cycles.

TAM · Total Addressable Market

The total revenue opportunity if you captured 100% of demand globally. Used in pitch decks to frame ambition.

SAM · Serviceable Addressable Market

The slice of TAM you can realistically serve given current product, geography, language, and channel reach.

SOM · Serviceable Obtainable Market

The slice of SAM you can realistically capture in the next few years given competition, sales capacity, and brand. The honest number.

MQL · Marketing Qualified Lead

A lead marketing has scored as ready for sales follow-up based on engagement signals like content downloads, demo requests, or pricing page visits.

SQL · Sales Qualified Lead

A lead sales has confirmed as worth active pursuit, typically after a discovery call validating fit, budget, and intent.

PQL · Product Qualified Lead

A user whose in-product behavior signals readiness to convert. The native lead type for PLG, where usage patterns trigger sales outreach.

SAL · Sales Accepted Lead

A lead sales has formally accepted from marketing for active follow-up. The handoff stage between MQL and SQL in a strict marketing-to-sales pipeline.

Sales Methodology
BANT · Budget, Authority, Need, Timeline

The classic four-question qualification framework from IBM. Fast and easy to teach. Many modern sellers consider it too transactional for complex deals.

MEDDIC / MEDDPICC · Metrics, Economic Buyer, Decision Criteria, Decision Process, Identify Pain, Champion (plus Paper Process and Competition)

The standard framework for complex enterprise deals. Forces sellers to map the buying organization in detail and earn a Champion who sells internally on your behalf.

SPIN · Situation, Problem, Implication, Need-payoff

Neil Rackham’s question framework for consultative selling. Move buyers from facts to felt pain to articulated value through a sequence of questions.

ABM · Account-Based Marketing

Marketing aimed at a defined list of target accounts with personalized campaigns and content, in tight coordination with sales.

GTM · Go-To-Market

The full strategy and motion for taking a product to customers, covering ICP, channels, pricing, packaging, sales process, and customer success.

Sales Roles
SDR · Sales Development Representative

Outbound prospector. Researches accounts, runs cold sequences, and books qualified meetings for AEs to close.

BDR · Business Development Representative

Often the inbound counterpart to an SDR, qualifying leads that come in through marketing. Some orgs use BDR and SDR interchangeably.

AE · Account Executive

The closer. Owns deals from qualified opportunity through signed contract. Carries a quota in ARR or ACV.

AM · Account Manager

Owns the customer relationship post-sale, focused on renewal and expansion across the existing book.

CSM · Customer Success Manager

Drives adoption, value realization, and account health. The role most responsible for NRR in modern SaaS orgs.

CRO · Chief Revenue Officer

Owns the entire revenue function across sales, marketing, and customer success. The first CRO hire often signals readiness to scale GTM at Series B and beyond.

Deal Documents
LOI · Letter of Intent

A document signaling intent to enter a deal, used as a soft commitment ahead of definitive agreements. Founders collect these as demand signals to show investors.

MOU · Memorandum of Understanding

An expression of alignment between parties on the shape of a future agreement. Used as a soft commitment ahead of formal contracting.

NDA · Non-Disclosure Agreement

A contract protecting confidential information shared between parties. Mutual NDAs cover both sides; one-way NDAs cover only the disclosing party.

MSA · Master Services Agreement

The umbrella legal contract governing an ongoing commercial relationship. Specific work attaches via SOWs, so future deals move faster.

SOW · Statement of Work

The specific scope, deliverables, timeline, and price for a defined engagement under an MSA. Where the actual work gets agreed on.

SLA · Service Level Agreement

Performance commitments such as uptime, response time, and support availability, often with penalties or credits attached if missed.

DPA · Data Processing Agreement

A contract addendum covering how customer data is processed, stored, and protected. Required for GDPR compliance and most enterprise security reviews.

POC · Proof of Concept

A scoped trial where the buyer validates the product against their specific use case. Usually time-bound with success criteria agreed upfront.

RFP · Request for Proposal

Formal procurement document where a buyer asks vendors to bid on a defined scope. Common in regulated industries and enterprise IT.

RFI · Request for Information

Early-stage procurement request to learn about vendor capabilities. Often precedes an RFP and helps the buyer narrow the field.

Marketing & Acquisition
CPL · Cost Per Lead

Total marketing spend divided by leads generated. The headline metric for top-of-funnel marketing efficiency.

CPA · Cost Per Acquisition

Total marketing spend divided by new customers, or whatever conversion event you define as the acquisition. A close cousin of CAC for paid channels.

CPC · Cost Per Click

Average cost paid per ad click. The atomic unit of paid search and most paid social buying.

CPM · Cost Per Mille

Cost per thousand ad impressions. The standard pricing model for awareness and brand campaigns.

CTR · Click-Through Rate

Clicks divided by impressions, expressed as a percentage. The headline engagement metric for ads, emails, and search snippets.

CVR · Conversion Rate

Conversions divided by visitors or clicks at a given funnel stage. Track per channel and per stage to find leaks.

ROAS · Return on Ad Spend

Revenue generated per dollar of ad spend. The performance marketing benchmark, typically targeting 3x to 5x for healthy paid programs.

SEO · Search Engine Optimization

The practice of earning organic search traffic through content depth, technical site quality, and backlinks. Compounds over years.

SEM · Search Engine Marketing

Paid search advertising, primarily through Google Ads. Often used loosely to include SEO, though most teams reserve it for paid.

PPC · Pay-Per-Click

Ad model where you pay only when someone clicks. The dominant pricing model for search ads and most performance social.

UTM · Urchin Tracking Module Parameters

URL parameters that tag traffic by source, medium, and campaign so you can attribute conversions in analytics tools.

Product & Engagement
DAU · Daily Active Users

Unique users active on a given day. The granular pulse metric for habit-forming consumer products.

WAU · Weekly Active Users

Unique users active in a rolling or fixed seven-day window. Useful for products used a few times a week, like collaboration tools.

MAU · Monthly Active Users

Unique users active in a thirty-day window. The default scale metric for consumer and freemium products.

DAU/MAU · Stickiness Ratio

DAU divided by MAU, expressed as a percentage. 20%+ signals real habitual usage; consumer social products target 50%+.

NPS · Net Promoter Score

Survey metric from -100 to +100 measuring willingness to recommend on a 0-10 scale. Promoters minus Detractors. Above 50 is excellent for B2B SaaS.

CSAT · Customer Satisfaction Score

Direct satisfaction rating from a customer interaction or experience, typically on a 1-5 or 1-7 scale.

CES · Customer Effort Score

Survey measuring how easy it was for a customer to accomplish their goal. Higher predictive power for retention than satisfaction scores.

TTV / TTFV · Time to Value / Time to First Value

How long from sign-up until a customer experiences meaningful value. TTFV focuses on the first activation moment, the leading indicator for retention.

MVP · Minimum Viable Product

The smallest version of a product that delivers learning about a hypothesis. The point is the learning, the speed is just a means to it.

Models & Motions
B2B · Business to Business

Selling to other companies. Defined by longer sales cycles, larger ACVs, and procurement processes.

B2C · Business to Consumer

Selling to individual end users. Defined by shorter purchase decisions, lower price points, and brand-driven acquisition.

B2B2C · Business to Business to Consumer

Selling to a business that delivers your product to consumers. Common for embedded fintech, white-label software, and platform plays.

D2C / DTC · Direct to Consumer

Brand sells directly to end users, owning the customer relationship and the channel. Common in e-commerce and CPG.

SaaS · Software as a Service

Software delivered over the cloud on a subscription model. The dominant venture-backable software business model of the past two decades.

PaaS · Platform as a Service

Cloud platforms developers build applications on top of, like Heroku, Vercel, and Render. Abstracts away infrastructure management.

IaaS · Infrastructure as a Service

Raw cloud compute, storage, and networking, like AWS EC2, GCP Compute, and Azure VMs.

PLG · Product-Led Growth

Growth motion where the product itself drives acquisition, activation, and expansion. Sales gets involved later for expansion and enterprise tiers.

SLG · Sales-Led Growth

Growth motion driven by sales team activity, with marketing supporting through demand gen. Standard for enterprise software with high ACVs.

CLG · Community-Led Growth

Growth motion built around an engaged user community as the primary acquisition and retention channel. Common in dev tools and creator platforms.
